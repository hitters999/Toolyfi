What began five days ago as a coordinated surprise military operation has rapidly escalated into the most significant armed conflict in the Middle East since the Gulf War. The United States and Israel, acting in concert under "Operation Epic Fury" and "Operation Roaring Lion" respectively, have launched wave after wave of precision strikes against Iranian military installations, nuclear sites, and government infrastructure.

⚠️ War Summary — Day 5

Operations began February 28, 2026. US B-2 bombers, F-22s, and carrier-based jets — alongside Israeli F-35Is — have conducted over 400 individual strike missions. Iran has retaliated with drones, ballistic missiles, and naval provocations.

How The War Started

On the night of February 28, 2026, without public warning, US and Israeli forces simultaneously launched the most complex coordinated air campaign since World War II. Dozens of stealth bombers and fighters crossed into Iranian airspace, striking air defense systems first to blind Iran's military radar network, then methodically destroying nuclear enrichment facilities at Fordow, Natanz, and Parchin.

The opening strikes lasted six hours. By dawn on March 1st, Iran's air force had been largely neutralized on the ground. Within 24 hours, Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was confirmed killed in a direct strike on his compound in Tehran — the most significant targeted killing in modern military history.

US Air Force F-15E Strike Eagle — one of the primary aircraft used in Operation Epic Fury. (USAF / Public Domain)

This is not a skirmish. This is a war designed to permanently end Iran's nuclear program and its ability to threaten the region.

— Senior US Defense Official, speaking to reporters in Washington

Khamenei Killed — Power Vacuum in Tehran

The death of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — 85 years old and the unchallenged ruler of Iran since 1989 — has sent shockwaves through the Iranian government. No succession plan was ever publicly announced. The Assembly of Experts, the body responsible for selecting a new Supreme Leader, has been unable to convene due to ongoing strikes.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) has effectively seized day-to-day control of the country. Iran's President has called for an emergency session of parliament, but with communications infrastructure destroyed across major cities, organizing any coordinated response has proven nearly impossible for Iranian authorities.

Iran's Retaliation

Despite suffering devastating losses, Iran has not stopped fighting. In the days since the war began, Iranian forces and their proxies have launched hundreds of drone and missile attacks across the region — targeting US military bases in Iraq, Syria, and Kuwait, as well as civilian infrastructure in Israel and Saudi Arabia.

Iranian Shahed-series drones — the primary weapon used in Iran's retaliatory strikes across the Gulf. (Wikimedia Commons)

US Embassies Targeted

Iranian drones struck the perimeters of US diplomatic compounds in Riyadh, Dubai, and Kuwait City. The State Department confirmed structural damage at two locations. Approximately 1,600 American diplomatic personnel have been evacuated from Gulf countries in emergency operations. US embassies in Kuwait, Lebanon, and Saudi Arabia are now closed.

Naval Confrontation Near Sri Lanka

In a dramatic escalation of the naval dimension of the conflict, a US submarine intercepted and sank the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena approximately 400 nautical miles southwest of Sri Lanka. The Iranian vessel had reportedly been shadowing a US carrier group. Sri Lanka's coast guard confirmed recovering bodies. Eighty Iranian sailors are confirmed killed.

The Strait of Hormuz Crisis

The Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of the world's oil supply passes — is now effectively closed to commercial shipping. (NASA / Public Domain)

The single most economically devastating consequence of the war so far is the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Iran mined key shipping lanes and deployed fast-attack boats to threaten any commercial vessel attempting transit. Lloyd's of London has suspended insurance coverage for Hormuz shipping, making passage commercially impossible for most tanker operators.

The result has been an immediate, violent spike in global energy prices. Oil jumped from $95/barrel before the war to $180/barrel within 48 hours — the steepest single-week rise in petroleum history. Gas prices in the United States rose 12 cents per gallon in a single day. Analysts warn prices could reach $220/barrel if the closure continues beyond two weeks.

Global Markets in Freefall

NYSE trading floor — stocks plunged at opening bell as war news rattled global investors. (Wikimedia Commons / Public Domain)

The Dow Jones Industrial Average shed more than 900 points at the opening bell on March 4th — its worst single-day drop since the COVID-19 pandemic crash of March 2020. The S&P 500 fell 3.2%. Asian and European markets had already closed sharply lower.

Safe-haven assets surged. Gold reached a record $3,200 per ounce. The US dollar strengthened sharply against most currencies. Defense contractor stocks — Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman — surged 8–12%.

The Human Cost

Beyond the geopolitical calculations, the human toll is mounting rapidly. Iranian health authorities, still partially functional, report 1,045 confirmed dead — predominantly military personnel and first responders, but including an unknown number of civilians in urban areas near targeted infrastructure. Thousands more are reported wounded, with Iran's hospital system under severe strain as power outages affect major medical facilities.

In Israel, two civilians were killed and fourteen injured when an Iranian ballistic missile penetrated the Iron Dome defense system and struck a residential neighborhood in Haifa. It was the first Iranian direct strike on Israeli soil in history — a moment Israeli officials called "a red line crossed."

What Happens Next

President Trump, in remarks to reporters, stated the military campaign would last "four to five weeks" and that the US objective is the "complete and permanent elimination of Iran's nuclear weapons capability." He rejected ceasefire calls from Russia, China, and the United Nations.

Military analysts warn the hardest phase of the conflict may still be ahead — particularly if Iran succeeds in activating Hezbollah in Lebanon for a northern front against Israel, or if Houthi forces in Yemen intensify attacks on Red Sea shipping. The war that started five days ago shows no signs of ending quickly.
